Lesson 88 | Teacher guidance Equilibrium

  • Worksheets will need to be printed for students in advance of the lesson.
  • While all care is taken to ensure resource content is in line with cultural sensitivities, please be aware that it should be reviewed before sharing with students.

Reversible processes

  • Place two clear bowls labelled R and P, on the bench. The one labelled R should be partly filled with water and the one labelled P should be left empty. Ask a student volunteer to transfer the water from one bowl to the other using a 250 cm3 plastic beaker. Pour the water back into bowl R and ask another student to do the same using a smaller beaker.
  • Discuss how the transfer of water models chemical reactions, with reactants (R) changing into products (P); the bowls representing reactant and products; and the different sized beakers representing different rates of reaction. Students should be able to see that this model shows:
    • A greater volume of water is moved initially, with the water transfer process then slowing down. This mimics the rate of a chemical reaction that is faster at the beginning and slows down at the end.
    • The different sized beakers should transfer water at different rates. The larger beaker transfers the water faster than the smaller beaker. This could be linked to chemical reactions having different rates due to surface area, concentration, temperature or use of a catalyst.
  • Introduce ideas about reversible processes. Ask students: Can you think of any processes which are reversible? (Answer: Typical answers could include melting chocolate, boiling water, melting ice.) Most of the ideas are likely to be changes of state and not actually reversible chemical reactions. Explain that chemical reactions can be reversible, especially if they take place in a closed or sealed container.
  • Then use two more volunteers (chosen carefully, as this could be messy) and give each a plastic beaker of equal size. Ask one student to start transferring water from R into the empty bowl P, as the other transfers water back again from P into R. At the start, more water is going from R to P than from P to R, because it is difficult to get a full beaker of water from a nearly empty bowl. The forward reaction is faster than the back reaction. After some time however, as the forward reaction slows and the reverse reaction speeds up, the reaction rates will become equal and the amount of water in R and P will stay the same.
  • Tell students:
    • This represents a reversible reaction. Equilibrium occurs when equal amounts are going both ways.
    • The term ‘dynamic equilibrium’ refers to reversible chemical reactions but we will look at this in greater detail during the lesson.

Dynamic equilibrium

  • Tell students:
    • In a reversible reaction, equilibrium is reached when the rate of the forward reaction equals the rate of the reverse reaction.
    • Equilibrium can only be reached if the reversible reaction takes place in apparatus where the reactants and products cannot escape (this is known as a closed system ).
    • At equilibrium, the volume, concentrations or percentages of each reactant and product do not change.
    • A more accurate description is dynamic equilibrium because the reactions are still ongoing (dynamic), but the reactants and products stay in balance (equilibrium).
    • Dynamic equilibrium only occurs when there is no loss of reactants or products.
  • Write the equation on the board: ammonium chloride ⇌ ammonia + hydrogen chloride and display Slide 1 of the slideshow.
  • Tell students:
    • This diagram shows a representation of dynamic equilibrium for the thermal decomposition of ammonium chloride.
    • Label 1 shows the concentration of ammonium chloride decreasing.
    • Label 2 shows the concentrations of ammonia and hydrogen chloride increasing.
    • Label 3 shows when dynamic equilibrium has been reached. What is happening at this point in the reaction? (Answer: The forward and backward reactions are still occurring but the percentages/amounts/volumes/masses of the reactants and products are no longer changing.)
  • Some students may need help with the idea of a ‘system’ and of closed and open systems.
  • It is often useful to consider everyday examples with students, such as the equilibrium between carbon dioxide gas and dissolved carbon dioxide in a fizzy drink:
    CO2(g) ⇌ CO2(aq). An equilibrium position is achieved when the stopper is closed (a closed system). Equilibrium cannot be achieved when the stopper is opened (an open system) as the carbon dioxide can escape and the drink goes ‘flat’.
  • Tell students:
    • Reactions in school science laboratories tend to be carried out in open systems, i.e., in beakers or test tubes without any coverings, so any gases could escape meaning equilibrium cannot be achieved.
    • Although many reactions are reversible, dynamic equilibrium only occurs in closed systems, where there is no loss of reactants or products.
    • In an open system, gases could escape and so equilibrium would not be achieved.

Equilibrium role-play

  • Hand out Worksheet 1 individually to students, but then arrange the class into large groups of approximately seven or eight students.
  • Tell students:
    • In these groups, you will develop a role-play about a reversible reaction, in both an open and a closed system.
    • Your role-play should demonstrate:
      • what happens in a reversible reaction
      • the difference between an open system and a closed system
      • what happens at equilibrium
      • what it means if this equilibrium lies to the left or to the right.
  • Give students a few minutes to plan and practise their role-play and then ask them to perform it. During the role-play you will need to narrate what is happening to those students watching.
  • Once students have completed the role-play, ask them to write in their exercise books an explanation of what they did and how it demonstrated the four key points outlined above.

Support

For students who struggle with Dynamic equilibrium:

  • In Student work 1, for the role-play, you may need to provide roles or suggestions. For example, students could be particles that are be trapped in a closed system allowing a reversible reaction to take place. Then they could show an open system where some particles can escape and therefore not return to the original reactants.

Interpreting dynamic equilibrium graphs

  • Display Slide 1 of the slideshow.
  • Tell students:
    • The graph represents the heating of ammonium chloride.
    • The reverse reaction [1] gets faster and at the same time the forward reaction [2] gets slower.
    • At a certain point in the reaction, [3], both forward and reverse reactions are occurring at the same rate.
    • It takes time to get to this point, and it is possible to determine this time from the x-axis [4]. This is the time it takes to reach dynamic equilibrium.
  • Explain to students: This graph shows equal concentrations of the reactants and products at dynamic equilibrium but this is not always the case.
  • Display Slide 2.
  • Ask students: Can you determine the mixture of reactants and products at dynamic equilibrium for this reaction? (Answer: 30% reactants and 70% products)

Alternative activities

  • These resources can be used if you are splitting the content of this lesson into two sessions.

    Reaction equations

  • Ask students: In your exercise books, write down two or three word and balanced equations for any reactions you can remember.
  • Put students into small groups to review their equations, making corrections where necessary and naming the different types of reactions.
  • Ask random groups to contribute examples to put on the board and allow students to comment on the equations written by other groups.
  • Explain that reactants are the starting materials for the chemical reaction and that the products are the substances that are produced in the reaction. Some of the products may not be needed or even wanted. A more efficient chemical reaction will produce fewer waste products. The arrow does not mean equal like it might do in mathematics. Instead, it means that a reaction has taken place and one or more substances have changed into something else.
  • Tell students: Don’t forget that a reaction might be reversible and this would be represented by the use of the double half arrow sign.
  • Some students may need to be given examples of reaction equations:
    • H2(g) + N2(g) ⇌ NH3(g)
      • Answer: 3H2(g) + N2(g) ⇌ 2NH3(g)
    • Mg (s) + O2(g) \(\rightarrow\) MgO(s)
      • Answer: 2Mg(s) + O2(g) \(\rightarrow\) 2MgO(s)

Quick quiz

  • Give students mini whiteboards.
  • Ask students: Write your answer down for each of the following questions:

    1. What does the term ‘equilibrium’ describe? (Answer: A position of balance.)
    2. What does the symbol ‘⇌’ mean? (Answer: Reversible reaction)
    3. What is it called when substances cannot enter or leave an observed environment, such as a stoppered test tube? (Answer: A closed system)
    4. What is it called when substances can enter or leave a system, such as a reaction inside an unstoppered test tube? (Answer: An open system)
  • Ask students to hold up their answer after a count of three before you tell them the correct answer. This will give a good overview of the class understanding. If they are not sure or do not have an answer, they should draw a question mark ‘?’ so that all students are made to respond. Try and address any issues that show up as they arise.
