In this lesson, students will learn to:

  • describe how to identify carbonate ions
  • describe how to identify sulfate ions in solution
  • describe how to identify halide ions in solution
  • describe a test for the presence of water.

Students should already be familiar with testing for positive ions from Lesson 35.

  • anhydrous: without water
  • carbonate ions: ions formed by carbon and oxygen
  • halide ions: ions formed by halogens
  • sulfate ions: ions formed by sulfur and water

Testing for halide ions


  1. Fill a test tube to a depth of about 2 cm with one of the solutions containing halide ions (Cl–, Br– or I– ions).
  2. Add a few drops of dilute nitric acid then shake the test tube to mix its contents.
  3. Add a few drops of silver nitrate solution. Record the colour of the precipitate produced.
  4. Repeat steps 1–3 with the other known solutions that contain halide ions (Cl–, Br– or I– ions).

    5. Testing for sulfate ions


  5. Fill a test tube to a depth of about 2 cm with the solution containing sulfate ions, SO42–.
  6. Add a few drops of dilute hydrochloric acid then shake the test tube to mix its contents.
  7. Add a few drops of barium chloride solution. Record the colour of the precipitate produced.

    8. Testing for carbonate ions


  8. Fill a test tube to a depth of about 2 cm with the solution containing carbonate ions, CO32–.
  9. Add a few drops of dilute nitric acid or dilute hydrochloric acid. Record whether bubbling occurs.

    10. Identifying anions in solution


  10. Fill a test tube to a depth of about 2 cm with one of the unknown solutions.
  11. Carry out a test for halide ions or sulfate ions. Also look for bubbling (and so the presence of carbonate ions) when you acidify the solution with dilute nitric acid or dilute hydrochloric acid.
  12. Repeat steps 10 and 11 until you have determined which anions each unknown solution contains.

Some students may struggle to answer analysis questions. A common error with analysis questions is for students to just jump to conclusions without reading the entire question and all the information provided. When confronted with an analysis question, encourage students to highlight points in the text or create a table with the following headings: name of test; result; meaning of result. For example:


Chemicals involved in test  Result  Meaning of test 
barium chloride  white precipitate  sulfate ion present 

This is because often the question provides the chemicals involved in the test as a means of identifying the ion(s) being tested for.
