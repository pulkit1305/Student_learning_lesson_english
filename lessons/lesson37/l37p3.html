Learning objectives

Learning objectives

In this lesson, students will learn to:

  • explain why the test for a given ion must be unique to that ion
  • recall some metal hydroxide precipitate colours
  • describe how to identify metal ions using sodium hydroxide solution
  • describe how to identify ammonium ions and ammonia.

Key definitions

Key definitions

  • anhydrous: without water
  • carbonate ions: ions formed by carbon and oxygen
  • halide ions: ions formed by halogens
  • sulfate ions: ions formed by sulfur and water

Teaching session 1

Negative ion tests

Carbonate test

  1. Add a little dilute hydrochloric acid to a test tube containing a small amount of an unknown sample.
  2. If bubbles are produced, bubble the gas through limewater.
  3. If the limewater turns cloudy white then carbon dioxide was produced and therefore your sample contained carbonate ions.





Sulfate test

  1. Make a solution of the sample with distilled water, add a small amount to a test tube.
  2. Add an equal volume of dilute hydrochloric acid to the sample to remove any other anions present.
  3. Add barium chloride solution.
  4. If a white precipitate is formed then sulfate ions are present in the sample.

Halide test

  1. Add a small amount of the sample to a test tube and add distilled water to make a solution.
  2. Add an equal volume of dilute nitric acid to the test tube.
  3. Add silver nitrate to the test tube.
  4. If halide ions are present a precipitate will form.

A white precipitate shows that chloride ions are present in the sample.

A cream precipitate shows that bromide ions are present.

A yellow precipitate shows that iodide ions are present.

Teaching session 2

Testing for water

  1. Add the unknown liquid to anhydrous copper sulfate which is white.
  2. Any sample that contains water will turn anhydrous copper sulfate blue.

Practice question

Exam-style question

Unknown substance X

Substance X gives a lilac flame test result and produces a white precipitate when silver nitrate is added. Name substance X.

Unknown substance Y

Substance Y does not give a colour during a flame test. When added to sodium hydroxide, a green precipitate is produced. When tested with acid and limewater, the limewater was turned milky white by a gas. Name substance Y.

Answers

X: potassium chloride (lilac flame test – potassium, white precipitate with silver nitrate – chlorine)

Y: iron carbonate (green precipitate – iron; limewater milky indicates carbon dioxide released from a carbonate)
