Lesson 37 | Teacher guidance Testing for Negative Ions and Water

  • Worksheets will need to be printed for students in advance of the lesson.
  • While all care is taken to ensure resource content is in line with cultural sensitivities, please be aware that it should be reviewed before sharing with students.

Identifying gases review

  • Ask students, in pairs, using paper or mini whiteboards, to write down the lab tests for as many gases as they can. For example, oxygen (glowing splint reignites), hydrogen (lighted splint, hydrogen explodes with a squeaky pop), carbon dioxide (turns limewater milky), ammonia (turns damp red litmus paper blue), chlorine (turns damp blue litmus paper red, then bleaches it white). They should compare their answers with those of another pair then feed back to the class.
  • Give less able students an example, such as: Chlorine turns damp blue litmus paper red, then bleaches it white.

Negative ion tests

  • Tell students: There are three more chemical tests that you need to know, but these are for negative ions or anions. Combined with the previous tests on positive ions (or cations), you should be able to determine the composition of an unknown metal salt when provided with certain pieces of information. You should also be able to recall the tests themselves.
  • Tell students: There are three distinct areas you need to develop your knowledge and understanding in:
    • tests for carbonate ions (and confirmatory test for carbon dioxide)
    • tests for sulfate ions
    • tests for halide ions.
  • If the time is available, it is worth showing students the tests, along with what they should be looking for. With the halide tests try to have some standards available to allow comparison because it can be difficult to distinguish between the colours.
  • Display Slide 1 of the Slideshow: Negative ion tests.
  • Tell students: When any acid is added to a salt that contains carbonate ions, carbon dioxide is produced. By testing the gas produced to determine whether it is carbon dioxide, you can determine if a carbonate ion is present in the salt.
  • Write the formula of the carbonate ion (CO32−) so students can see it is a negative ion.
  • Demonstrate the reaction of calcium carbonate with dilute hydrochloric acid to form bubbles of carbon dioxide gas. Add a delivery tube to lead off the gas formed so it can be tested using limewater in a boiling tube.
  • Ask students: Why do we need to test the gas produced? (Answer: The limewater test is particularly important when an unknown solid is being tested, because it is possible that the solid could contain a reactive metal that produces hydrogen instead of carbon dioxide.)
  • Ask more able students: Why does carbon dioxide form a white precipitate with limewater (calcium hydroxide solution)? (Answer: A precipitate of calcium carbonate is formed: Ca(OH)2(aq) + CO2(g) \(\rightarrow\;\) CaCO3(s) + H2O(l).)
  • Display Slide 2 of the slideshow.
  • Tell students: The test for sulfate ions involves using barium chloride solution to form a white precipitate. The positively charged barium ions form an insoluble compound with the negatively charged sulfate ions.
  • Write the formula of the sulfate ion (SO42−) so students can see it is a negative ion.
  • Demonstrate the sulfate ion test.
  • Ask students: Can you suggest a reason why dilute sulfuric acid is not used to acidify the sample when testing for sulfate ions? (Answer: Sulfuric acid contains sulfate ions; which would react with barium chloride/barium ions to form barium sulfate; giving a false positive test for sulfate ions in the sample.)
  • Display Slide 3 of the slideshow.
  • Tell students: The final group of negative ions you need to know how to test for are the halides. The halides are the ions formed by the halogens. Testing for halides produces precipitates that form slightly different colours.
  • Write the formulas of the halide ions (Cl−, Br−, l−) so students can see they are negative ions.
  • Demonstrate the halide ion test.
  • Ask students: Can you suggest a reason why fluoride ions cannot be detected in the same way as chloride, bromide, and iodide ions? (Answer: Silver fluoride is soluble/does not form a precipitate.)

Student practical 1: Testing for negative ions

  • Hand out Worksheet 1: Practical method to students. Ask students to work in pairs – they will still need a worksheet each.
  • Tell students to work in pairs and use the various techniques just shown for identifying negative ions.
  • Direct students to use silver nitrate solution to identify Cl−, Br− and I− ions; barium chloride solution to detect sulfate ions; and dilute acid to detect carbonate ions in sodium carbonate solution via effervescence. They should then identify the negatively charged ions present in the unknown samples.
  • You may wish to set each known solution at five or ten stations around the lab to avoid cross-contamination and run the first part of the activity as a circus. Once the expected results have been identified and agreed upon, the students could then test the five unknown solutions.
  • Check that the students appreciate that they can use either acid for their carbonate test, but they must use dilute nitric acid with silver nitrate and dilute hydrochloric acid with barium chloride solution.
  • Expected results: Chloride salts produce a white precipitate, bromide salts produce a cream precipitate and iodide salts produce a yellow precipitate. Sulfate salts produce a white precipitate and carbonate salts produce bubbles of carbon dioxide gas (which turns limewater milky).
  • If there is not enough time for all students to complete tests on all the unknown samples, each pair could just test one or two unknowns, then pool the class results.

Notes on equipment

  • It helps if the salts are simply labelled with their non-metal ions instead of their full names.

Testing for halide ions


  1. Fill a test tube to a depth of about 2 cm with one of the solutions containing halide ions (Cl–, Br– or I– ions).
  2. Add a few drops of dilute nitric acid then shake the test tube to mix its contents.
  3. Add a few drops of silver nitrate solution. Record the colour of the precipitate produced.
  4. Repeat steps 1–3 with the other known solutions that contain halide ions (Cl–, Br– or I– ions).

    5. Testing for sulfate ions


  5. Fill a test tube to a depth of about 2 cm with the solution containing sulfate ions, SO42–.
  6. Add a few drops of dilute hydrochloric acid then shake the test tube to mix its contents.
  7. Add a few drops of barium chloride solution. Record the colour of the precipitate produced.

    8. Testing for carbonate ions


  8. Fill a test tube to a depth of about 2 cm with the solution containing carbonate ions, CO32–.
  9. Add a few drops of dilute nitric acid or dilute hydrochloric acid. Record whether bubbling occurs.

    10. Identifying anions in solution


  10. Fill a test tube to a depth of about 2 cm with one of the unknown solutions.
  11. Carry out a test for halide ions or sulfate ions. Also look for bubbling (and so the presence of carbonate ions) when you acidify the solution with dilute nitric acid or dilute hydrochloric acid.
  12. Repeat steps 10 and 11 until you have determined which anions each unknown solution contains.
  • Eye protection should be worn.
  • Avoid skin contact with the substances used.
  • Barium chloride solution is harmful.
  • Dilute nitric acid is an irritant.

Support

For students who struggle with Negative ion tests:

  • In Student practical 1: Testing for negative ions:
    • provide them with a mnemonic for the correct acid for the sulfate and halide ion tests, e.g., hydroCHLoric acid for barium CHLoride; NITric acid for silver NITrate
    • provide them with a mnemonic for silver halide colours, e.g., milk, cream, butter (white, cream, yellow) going down Group 7
    • provide them with the table; parts of the table could be omitted for them to fill in, or it could be provided completed for them to refer to if they are questioned about the tests in the plenary. Students could also use this summary table to help them with the homework.

Testing for water

  • Tell students: The final set of tests is to detect water. There are two tests that can be carried out.
  • Display Slide 1 of the slideshow.
  • Ask students: Can you think of any tests that might indicate if a substance is water? (Answer: Measure the pH using a pH probe or indicator; measure the melting and boiling points.)
  • Tell students: We can test the purity of water by using a physical test. Pure water boils at exactly 100 °C and freezes at exactly 0 °C. Therefore, if the boiling point or melting point of your sample is different to either of these two temperatures then your sample is not pure.
  • In reality, even if the water is pure it can be difficult to obtain the exact temperatures quoted above and practically it can be difficult to measure these accurately.
  • It is enough for students to be aware of the fixed points for pure water and the use of anhydrous copper sulfate in testing for water.
  • Other options could be to heat a sample to dryness on a pre-weighed evaporating basin. When re-weighed it is possible to determine if any dissolved solids are left behind in the basin. However, the specification states, ‘describe a physical test to show whether a sample of water is pure’ and this is simply to check its boiling (or melting) point.

Support

For students who struggle with Testing for water:

  • In Student work 2, in Worksheet 2, leave the grid on the sheet for students to use and add one or two more answers.
  • Ask students to answer the following questions:
    • Write a balanced ionic equation to show the reaction between aqueous hydrogen ions (produced by acids in solution) and aqueous carbonate ions to form carbon dioxide gas and water. Include state symbols. (Answer: 2H+(aq) + CO32− (aq) → CO2(g) + H2O(l))
    • Write an ionic equation, including state symbols, for the reaction between barium ions and sulfate ions. (Answer: Ba2+(aq) + SO42− (aq) → BaSO4(s))
    • Write an ionic equation for the reaction between silver ions and bromide ions. Include state symbols. (Answer: Ag+(aq) + Br− (aq) → AgBr(s))

Alternative activities

  • These resources can be used if you are splitting the content of this lesson into two sessions.

Demonstration practical: Lead halide precipitates

  • Demonstrate the reaction between lead nitrate solution and potassium bromide solution.
  • Discuss with students: How could these precipitates be useful in a chemical test to identify bromide ions and iodide ions in substances? (Answer: If the precipitate is cream in colour, bromide ions are present. If the precipitate is yellow in colour, iodide ions are present.)
  • Ask students: Could this be extended to chloride ions? (Answer: Yes, and a white precipitate forms.)
  • Remind students that potassium ions are present in both test solutions, so the different coloured precipitates must be due to the bromide ions and chloride ions (which react with the lead ions to form precipitates).
  • eye protection
  • 1 mol/dm3 potassium bromide
  • 1 mol/dm3 lead(II) nitrate solution
  • 1 mol/dm3 potassium iodide solution
  • two test tubes
  • test tube rack

  1. Add 0.1 mol/dm3 potassium bromide solution to a depth of about 2 cm in a test tube.
  2. Add a few drops of 0.1 mol/dm3 lead(II) nitrate solution. (A cream precipitate forms.)
  3. Repeat the demonstration, but using 0.1 mol/dm3 potassium iodide solution. (A yellow precipitate forms.)
  • Eye protection should be worn.
  • Lead(II) nitrate solution is toxic.

Confidence rating

  • Display Slide 1 of the Slideshow: Learning objectives.
  • Give the students 3–4 minutes to rate their confidence on each objective using a scale of 1 to 3, where 1 is not confident, 2 is unsure in places and 3 is confident.
  • Ask students to discuss their ratings with a partner. Walk round the class and discuss the ratings with a random selection of students, taking a note of any students who still seem very unsure.
  • Encourage students to produce a single page showing all the tests plus their results, to keep as a revision aid.
