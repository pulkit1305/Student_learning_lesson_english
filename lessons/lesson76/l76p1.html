Lesson 76 | Overview Acid Rain

4.14
know that, in car engines, the temperature reached is high enough to allow nitrogen and oxygen from air to react, forming oxides of nitrogen
4.15
explain how the combustion of some impurities in hydrocarbon fuels results in the formation of sulfur dioxide
4.16
understand how sulfur dioxide and oxides of nitrogen contribute to acid rain

In this lesson, students will learn to:

  • explain why nitrogen is inert, but that it will react in an engine
  • explain how sulfur dioxide can be formed during combustion
  • state how sulfur and nitrogen oxides form acid rain
  • describe some of the consequences of acid rain.

Students should already be familiar with the pH scale from their work in Lesson 50 and oxidation reactions from their work in Lesson 64.

  • acid rain: rain which has a pH of less than about 5.6; it is caused when water and oxygen in the atmosphere react with sulfur dioxide to produce sulfuric acid or with various oxides of nitrogen, NOx, to give nitric acid
  • impurities: unwanted substances found mixed into a useful substance
  • pollutant: a substance that harms living organisms when released into the environment
  • scrubbing: removing pollutant gases from the gases produced in a combustion reaction
  • weathering: when rocks are broken up by physical, chemical or biological processes

Demonstration practical: Burning sulfur (found in Starter)

  • eye protection
  • 0.3 g sulfur
  • deflagrating spoon with lid
  • Bunsen burner
  • heat-proof mat
  • gas jar of oxygen with lid
  • universal indicator solution
  1. Heat no more than 0.3 g of sulfur in a deflagrating spoon until it melts, then ignites.
  2. Place the spoon in a gas jar of oxygen, making sure that the deflagrating spoon lid covers the mouth of the gas jar.
  3. Observe a blue flame and white clouds being produced.
  4. When the reaction is complete and it has all cooled down, remove the spoon, add water to the jar and replace the gas jar lid.
  5. Shake carefully to mix.
  • Wear eye protection.
  • Carry out the demonstration in a fume cupboard or ensure adequate ventilation in the laboratory.
  • Sulfur dioxide gas is toxic and has a choking smell. It can also trigger asthma attacks – avoid excessive escape from the gas jar.
  • OPTIONAL: If the demonstration cannot be carried out, display Slide 1 of the Slideshow: Burning sulfur (found in Starter).

Student practical: Building materials and acid (found in Student work 1)

  • eye protection
  • test tube
  • test-tube rack
  • dropping pipette
  • substances to test (see below)
  • sieve and bowl for waste materials
  • 0.5 mol/dm3 sulfuric acid
  1. Put a small sample of a building material into the test tube.
  2. Add dilute sulfuric acid until the test tube is about one-third full.
  3. Observe and record what happens over the next 2 minutes, then wash out your test tube as directed by your teacher.
  4. Repeat steps 1–3 with other building materials.
  • Wear eye protection as 0.5 mol/dm3 sulfuric acid and 0.4 mol/dm3 nitric acid are irritants.
  • Take care not to jam the samples in the test tube.
  • To avoid blocking the sinks, pour the used contents of the test tubes into a sieve and bowl.
  • Some students may need help understanding that the sulfur impurities are present naturally, rather than being deliberately added. For example, the amino acids cysteine and methionine contain sulfur, so proteins in plants and animals naturally contain sulfur. Fossil fuels are made from the remains of once-living organisms.
