Burning sulfur

  • Carry out the Demonstration practical: Burning sulfur.

Demonstration practical: Burning sulfur

  • Before you begin, warn students that this will produce a very bright light.
  • eye protection
  • 0.3 g sulfur
  • deflagrating spoon with lid
  • Bunsen burner
  • heat-proof mat
  • gas jar of oxygen with lid
  • universal indicator solution
  1. Heat no more than 0.3 g of sulfur in a deflagrating spoon until it melts, then ignites.
  2. Place the spoon in a gas jar of oxygen, making sure that the deflagrating spoon lid covers the mouth of the gas jar.
  3. Observe a blue flame and white clouds being produced.
  4. When the reaction is complete and it has all cooled down, remove the spoon, add water to the jar and replace the gas jar lid.
  5. Shake carefully to mix.
  • Wear eye protection.
  • Carry out the demonstration in a fume cupboard or ensure adequate ventilation in the laboratory.
  • Sulfur dioxide gas is toxic and has a choking smell. It can also trigger asthma attacks – avoid excessive escape from the gas jar.
  • OPTIONAL: If the demonstration cannot be carried out, display Slide 1 of the Slideshow: Burning sulfur.

  • At the end of the demonstration, ask students:
    • Can you predict what will happen when universal indicator solution is added?
      (Answer: A red colour should be seen as an acidic oxide has been made.)
  • Add a few drops of universal indicator solution to the solution in the gas jar and observe the mixture turning red.

Rainwater and acid rain

  • Display Slide 1 of the Slideshow: Rainwater and acid rain.
  • Ask students: What are the four main gases in the air? (Answer: nitrogen, oxygen, argon, and carbon dioxide.)
  • Tell students:
    • Carbon dioxide, like other non-metal oxides which are soluble, can dissolve in water producing an acidic solution.
    • As a result of carbon dioxide in the air, rainwater is naturally acidic, around pH5.6. However, it is possible for rainwater to become more acidic. When this happens it is called acid rain.
  • Tell students:
    • There are two major causes of acid rain:
      • water and oxygen in the atmosphere reacting with sulfur dioxide to produce sulfuric acid
      • the reaction of oxides of nitrogen with water and oxygen in the atmosphere to produce nitric acid.
  • Display Slide 2 and play the video included in this slide.
  • Display Slide 3.
  • Tell students: Sulfur compounds occur naturally in hydrocarbon fuels, like gasoline or diesel.
  • Ask students: In your exercise books, write a balanced equation for the reaction between sulfur and oxygen.
  • Display Slide 4, which students can use to self-assess.
  • Display Slide 5.
  • Tell students: Sulfur dioxide dissolves in the water in clouds to form a weak acid, known as sulfurous acid.
  • Before discussing some of the issues with acid rain, tell students: Working in pairs, I want you to write down your own ideas about the impact of acid rain. (Possible answers: Acidifies rivers/lakes/soil; increases rate of weathering of stone; increases rate of corrosion of metals; harms living things/trees/fish/aquatic insects.)
  • Ask some of the pairs of students to feed back to the class. As they do so, students should create a mind map of the confirmed and corrected impacts of acid rain in their exercise books.
  • The impacts noted down should include those listed on Slide 6.
  • Display Slide 7.
  • Tell students: The stonework on the right is damaged by years of acid rain.
  • Display Slide 8.
  • Tell students:
    • The other major cause of acid rain is the reaction of oxides of nitrogen with the atmosphere.
    • As there are several oxides of nitrogen involved, they are given the general formula, NOx.
    • Like sulfur dioxide, oxides of nitrogen mainly come from power stations and factories burning fossil fuels. However, oxides of nitrogen are also formed from motor vehicles.
  • Display Slide 9.
  • Tell students:
    • Motor vehicles have internal combustion engines, meaning fuel is ignited inside the engine.
    • This results in very high temperatures which are high enough for nitrogen to be able to react with oxygen. This produces nitrogen monoxide.
  • Ask students:
    • Write the following question in your exercise books: Explain why oxides of nitrogen may be produced when hydrocarbon fuels are used.
    • Working in pairs, I want you to write an answer in your books. (Answer: Nitrogen and oxygen; in air; react together; in the high temperatures; inside engines/furnaces.)
  • Display Slide 10.
  • Tell students: Oxides of nitrogen, NOx, are a cause of acid rain. In clouds, nitrogen dioxide dissolves in water forming dilute nitric acid. The reaction is quite complex.
  • Ask students:
    • Catalytic converters can reduce nitrogen dioxide, forming nitrogen and oxygen.
    • Write a balanced equation for this reaction.
  • Students should write their answer in their exercise books.
  • If they are struggling, tell them: Start by writing a word equation (nitrogen dioxide → nitrogen + oxygen), replace the substances with symbols and finally attempt to balance it. (Answer: 2NO2 → N2 + 2O2)
  • Tell students: Sulfur dioxide and oxides of nitrogen are known as ‘atmospheric pollutants’. This is the term given to substances that when released into the environment harm living organisms.

Student practical: Building materials and acid

  • Tell students they will be investigating the effects of adding various building materials to dilute sulfuric acid and recording their observations. This reinforces the work on the impact of acid rain. Ask students to follow the method provided on Worksheet 1: Practical method.
  • There are likely to be no visible reactions with brick and copper, and slow reactions with zinc, steel and aluminium. Rapid bubbling may be observed with limestone, marble and concrete.
  • If there is sufficient time, students can repeat the experiment using dilute nitric acid (to mimic the effect of acid rain from NOx).
  • eye protection
  • test tube
  • test-tube rack
  • dropping pipette
  • substances to test (see below)
  • sieve and bowl for waste materials
  • 5 mol/dm3 sulfuric acid

Notes on equipment

  • Substances to test, for example small chips of building materials (limestone, marble, brick, concrete), small pieces of metal foil (zinc, aluminium, copper) and steel nails
  • Optional: 0.4 mol/dm3 nitric acid
  1. Put a small sample of a building material into the test tube.
  2. Add dilute sulfuric acid until the test tube is about one-third full.
  3. Observe and record what happens over the next 2 minutes, then wash out your test tube as directed by your teacher.
  4. Repeat steps 1–3 with other building materials.
  • Wear eye protection as 0.5 mol/dm3 sulfuric acid and 0.4 mol/dm3 nitric acid are irritants.
  • Take care not to jam the samples in the test tube.
  • To avoid blocking the sinks, pour the used contents of the test tubes into a sieve and bowl.

Support

For students who struggle with Rainwater and acid rain:

  • In Student work 1, for Student practical: Building materials and acid, make sure they understand the need to observe each material for a few minutes rather than just recording the immediate results.
  • Write the following question on the board for any student wishing to complete the Challenge activity. They can write the question at the bottom of Worksheet 1: Practical method and answer it underneath.
  • Why might the problems caused by acid rain vary over time?
  • Collect the worksheets in for marking and return next lesson if there is no time in this lesson.
  • (Possible answers: Acid rain is caused by acidic oxides/sulfur dioxide/oxides of nitrogen dissolving in rain water; release of sulfur dioxide depends upon fuel use/removal of sulfur at the oil refinery; release of oxides of nitrogen depends upon fuel use/use of catalytic converters; effects of acid rain can be reduced using limestone/calcium hydroxide; to neutralise excess acid.)
  • Display Question 1.
  • Ask students to work on their own to complete the question in their exercise books. Allow 2–3 minutes.
  • Ask students to swap over their books with their partner.
  • Click to reveal the answer, which students should use to mark each other’s work.
  • Before returning the books to their partner, ask students to consider the answer which they have marked. Ask students to add a WWW (What Went Well) comment to praise their partner and an EBI (Even Better If) to help their partner improve their answer.
  • Hand out the Homework sheet, which contains questions covering material from this and the last few lessons.
  • Q1 covers crude oil.
  • Q2 covers fractional distillation.
  • Q3 covers acid rain.

Alternative activities

  • These resources can be used if you are splitting the content of this lesson into two sessions.

Quick quiz

  • Give students mini whiteboards.
  • Ask students: Write your answer down for each of the following questions:

    1. Which elements are present in hydrocarbon molecules? (Answer: Hydrogen, carbon)
    2. Which gas reacts with hydrocarbons when they burn? (Answer: Oxygen/O2)
    3. Name a gas produced when carbon burns. (Answer: Carbon monoxide/carbon dioxide)
    4. What compound forms when hydrogen burns in air? (Answer: Water)
  • Ask students to hold up their answer after a count of three before you tell them the correct answers. This will give a good overview of the class understanding. Try and address any issues that are showing up as they arise.

Confidence rating

  • Display Slide 1 of the Slideshow: Learning objectives.
  • Give the students 3–4 minutes to rate their confidence on each objective using a scale of 1 to 3 where 1 is not confident, 2 is unsure in places and 3 is confident.
  • Ask students to discuss their ratings with a partner. Walk round the class and discuss the ratings with a random selection of students, taking a note of any students who still seem unsure.
