Hydrocarbons as fuels

  • Display Slide 1 of the slideshow.
  • Tell students:
    • As we have seen earlier in the topic, when hydrocarbons burn in air (oxygen) in combustion reactions, they produce carbon dioxide and water and release a lot of heat energy.
    • Combustion is a type of oxidation reaction.
  • Display Slide 2.
  • Tell students:
    • When there is plenty of air or oxygen, complete combustion occurs.
    • During complete combustion, only carbon dioxide and water are produced, and energy is given out.
    • For example, methane, CH4, from the refinery gases fraction reacts with oxygen to produce carbon dioxide and water.
  • It is possible to demonstrate the products of combustion using a candle (or a mini Bunsen burner). The apparatus used is shown in Slide 3.
  • Carry out Demonstration practical: Products of combustion and use anhydrous copper(II) sulfate or dry cobalt chloride paper to detect water in the U-shaped tube and limewater to detect carbon dioxide. Show the production of soot on the inverted filter funnel. (Soot is produced during incomplete combustion, which will be discussed in more detail in Teaching session 2.)

Demonstration practical: Products of combustion

  • eye protection
  • candle (or mini Bunsen burner)
  • heat-resistant mat
  • glass funnel
  • rubber tubing
  • U-shaped tube containing anhydrous copper(II) sulfate or dry cobalt chloride paper
  • boiling tube containing limewater
  • beaker large enough to accommodate the U-shaped tube
  • ice
  • forceps
  • pump

Notes on equipment

  • The funnel is connected to one side of the U-shaped tube.
  • The boiling tube is connected to the other side of the U-shaped tube, and leads to a pump.
  • Ice is needed to make an iced water bath in the beaker.
  • The mini Bunsen burner air hole should be fully open to demonstrate complete combustion, and fully closed for incomplete combustion.
  • Take care if using the mini Bunsen burner to avoid melting the delivery tube and funnel.
  1. Demonstrate the products of combustion of candle wax (or of methane gas if using the mini Bunsen burner), using apparatus similar to that shown on Slide 3 of the Slideshow: Hydrocarbons as fuels.
  2. Use anhydrous copper(II) sulfate or dry cobalt chloride paper to detect water in the U-shaped tube, and limewater to detect carbon dioxide.
  3. Show the production of soot on the inverted filter funnel.
  4. Show and discuss the differences seen with complete combustion (water and carbon dioxide only [limewater turns milky]) and incomplete combustion (water, but also black soot and little or no carbon dioxide [limewater less or not at all milky]).
  5. Discuss the hazard of carbon monoxide and the problems of gas and soot from appliances.
  • Wear eye protection. Anhydrous copper(II) sulfate is harmful and irritating to the eyes and skin.
  • Cobalt chloride is toxic: use forceps to handle the dry cobalt chloride paper, if this is used.
  • Make sure the students appreciate that the cold water outside the U-shaped tube does not enter the apparatus, but cools the water vapour inside.
  • Ask students questions about each of the parts of the apparatus:
    • Why is a glass funnel used? (Answer: Plastic would melt; traps any soot produced.)
    • What is being tested for in the U-shaped tube? (Answer: Water vapour)
    • Why is iced water needed around the U-shaped tube? (Answer: To cool the water vapour so it becomes water; it makes it easier for the anhydrous copper sulfate to become hydrated copper sulfate as the vapour might pass through too quickly to cause any change in the anhydrous copper sulfate.)
    • What is the expected colour change of anhydrous copper sulfate? (Answer: It is white when copper sulfate is in the anhydrous form and becomes hydrated blue copper sulfate if there is any water present.)
    • What is being tested for with the limewater? (Answer: Carbon dioxide)
    • What is the positive result for carbon dioxide with the limewater? (Answer: The limewater turns milky white/cloudy.)
    • Discuss the hazard of carbon monoxide and the problems of gas and soot from appliances. (Answer: If insufficient oxygen is present, then soot will form on objects. Soot can block pipes carrying waste gases from an appliance, blacken buildings and cause breathing problems; carbon monoxide, which is toxic, can be produced; it combines with haemoglobin in red blood cells preventing oxygen from combining with the haemoglobin; so reduces amount of oxygen carried in the bloodstream; causing affected people to feel sleepy or drowsy; this can lead to unconsciousness and even death.)
  • Tell students: The principal of complete combustion is used in a Bunsen burner when the air hole is open. The natural gas, which is largely methane, can completely combust producing only carbon dioxide and water with no residue.

Incomplete combustion

  • Tell students:
    • When there is a limited supply of air or oxygen, incomplete combustion occurs.
    • During incomplete combustion:
      • water is produced
      • energy is given out, but less than with complete combustion
      • carbon monoxide and/or carbon are produced.
    • The reason for the change in products is that when there is insufficient oxygen, the carbon is only partially oxidised, forming carbon monoxide. Carbon monoxide is a carbon with only one oxygen atom.
    • It is even possible that the carbon atoms are released without being bonded to anything; this would be seen as smoke or soot.
    • Various issues are caused by incomplete combustion.
  • Display Slide 1 of the slideshow.
  • To check understanding, ask students: Explain in your own words why the incomplete combustion of hydrocarbons can produce carbon monoxide and carbon. (Answer: There is a limited supply of oxygen; so carbon is not oxidised and is instead released as carbon (soot); or carbon is partially oxidised so is and is instead released as carbon monoxide.) Obtain a selection of answers randomly around the class.
  • Display Slide 2.
  • Tell students:
    • Carbon is seen as black soot or smoke. It can block pipes carrying waste gases from an appliance, blacken buildings and cause breathing problems.
    • Soot is also responsible for a phenomenon known as global dimming. This is the reduction in light reaching the Earth’s surface, thereby reducing light available for photosynthesis.
  • To check understanding, ask students: Describe in your own words one problem caused by soot. (Answer: Blocks pipes/blackens buildings/causes breathing problems.) Obtain a selection of answers randomly around the class.
  • Display Slide 3.
  • Tell students:
    • Carbon monoxide is a colourless gas that is toxic.
    • Carbon monoxide binds more strongly to haemoglobin in red blood cells than oxygen does.
    • So it prevents oxygen from combining with the haemoglobin and therefore reduces the amount of oxygen that can be carried in the bloodstream.
    • This can result in a person feeling sleepy; however, it can also lead to unconsciousness and even death.
  • To check understanding, ask students: Explain in your own words one problem caused by carbon monoxide. (Answer: Carbon monoxide combines with haemoglobin; stopping oxygen combining with haemoglobin; which reduces the amount of oxygen carried in the bloodstream.) Obtain a selection of answers randomly around the class.
  • Display Slide 4.
  • Tell students: As carbon monoxide is odourless and colourless, electronic carbon monoxide detectors are used in homes which rely on natural gas for heating and/or cooking.

Alternative activities

  • These resources can be used if you are splitting the content of this lesson into two sessions.

Quick quiz

  • Give students mini whiteboards and ask them to write their answer down for each of the following questions:

    1. Which elements are present in hydrocarbon molecules? (Answer: Hydrogen, carbon)
    2. Which gas reacts with hydrocarbons when they burn? (Answer: Oxygen/O2)
    3. Name a gas produced when carbon burns. (Answer: Carbon monoxide, carbon dioxide)
    4. What compound forms when hydrogen burns in air? (Answer: Water)
  • Ask students to hold up their answer after a count of three. This will give a good overview of the class understanding. Try and address any issues that are showing up as they arise.

Confidence rating

  • Ask students to discuss their ratings with a partner. Walk round the class and discuss the ratings with a random selection of students, taking a note of any students who still seem unsure.
