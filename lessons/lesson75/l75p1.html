Lesson 75 | Overview Hydrocarbons as Fuels

4.11
know that a fuel is a substance that, when burned, releases heat energy
4.12
know the possible products of complete and incomplete combustion of hydrocarbons with oxygen in the air
4.13
understand why carbon monoxide is poisonous, in terms of its effect on the capacity of blood to transport oxygen (references to haemoglobin are not required)

In this lesson, students will learn to:

  • state the meaning of the term ‘fuel’
  • state that full (complete) combustion of hydrocarbons releases carbon dioxide and water
  • state the conditions leading to incomplete combustion
  • explain why carbon monoxide forms in incomplete combustion
  • explain why carbon monoxide is toxic.

Students should already be familiar with different combustion reactions from their work in Lesson 72.

  • complete combustion: occurs when a hydrocarbon burns in sufficient oxygen; carbon dioxide and water are formed as products
  • incomplete combustion: occurs when a hydrocarbon burns in insufficient oxygen; water is still formed as a product, but carbon monoxide and carbon are formed instead of carbon dioxide

Demonstration practical: Products of combustion (found in Teaching session 1)

  • eye protection
  • candle (or mini Bunsen burner)
  • heat-resistant mat
  • glass funnel
  • rubber tubing
  • U-shaped tube containing anhydrous copper(II) sulfate or dry cobalt chloride paper
  • boiling tube containing limewater
  • beaker large enough to accommodate the U-shaped tube
  • ice
  • forceps
  • pump
  1. Demonstrate the products of combustion of candle wax (or of methane gas if using the mini Bunsen burner), using apparatus similar to that shown on Slide 3 of the slideshow.
  2. Use anhydrous copper(II) sulfate or dry cobalt chloride paper to detect water in the U-shaped tube, and limewater to detect carbon dioxide.
  3. Show the production of soot on the inverted filter funnel.
  4. Show and discuss the differences seen with complete combustion (water and carbon dioxide only [limewater turns milky]) and incomplete combustion (water, but also black soot, and little or no carbon dioxide [limewater less or not at all milky]).
  5. Discuss the hazard of carbon monoxide and the problems of gas and soot from appliances.
  • Wear eye protection. Anhydrous copper(II) sulfate is harmful and irritating to the eyes and skin. Cobalt chloride is toxic: use forceps to handle the dry cobalt chloride paper, if this is used.
  • Some students may need help understanding that water is always produced during combustion of hydrocarbons and alcohols, and that carbon dioxide can still be produced during incomplete combustion (but carbon monoxide or carbon cannot be produced during complete combustion). Demonstration practical: Products of combustion in Teaching session 1 should go some way to helping students with this misconception.
