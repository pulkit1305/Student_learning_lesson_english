Lesson 74 | Overview Fractions

4.8
describe how the industrial process of fractional distillation separates crude oil into fractions
4.9
know the names and uses of the main fractions obtained from crude oil: refinery gases, gasoline, kerosene, diesel, fuel oil and bitumen
4.10
know the trend in colour, boiling point and viscosity of the main fractions

In this lesson, students will learn to:

  • describe how fractional distillation is used to separate crude oil into fractions
  • state the names of the fractions in order
  • explain how molecular size determines the fraction in which the molecule appears
  • describe the trends in the fractions
  • relate the uses of the fractions to their properties.

Students should already be familiar with the process of fractional distillation from their work in the previous lesson and intermolecular forces from their work in Lesson 23.

  • fuel: a substance that when burned in oxygen releases heat energy
  • refinery gases: a mixture of methane, ethane, propane, and butane; this mixture is commonly used as liquefied petroleum gas (LPG) for domestic heating and cooking

Demonstration practical: Separating crude oil (found in Teaching session 1)

  • eye protection
  • side-arm boiling tube
  • thermometer in a bung (0–350 °C)
  • delivery tube
  • four test tubes with stoppers
  • test-tube rack
  • 250 cm3 beaker
  • stand, boss and clamp
  • Bunsen burner
  • heat-resistant mat
  • dropping pipette
  • watch glass
  • splints
  • glass rod
  • mineral wool
  • crude oil substitute

Distilling the crude oil substitute

  1. Use a glass rod to push a small ball of mineral wool into the bottom of the boiling tube. Add approximately 2 cm3 of crude oil substitute using the dropping pipette. Set up the apparatus as shown in the diagram. Make sure everything is secure before starting heating.
  2. Gently heat the bottom of the boiling tube using a small blue flame. Check the thermometer to make sure the temperature does not go above 100 °C. Liquid should collect in the test tube. When this stops, replace the test tube with a fresh one, and stopper the tube of liquid. Repeat the previous step three times, heating the boiling tube to about 150 °C, then to about 200 °C and finally to about 250 °C.
  3. The last step requires careful control of the Bunsen burner flame to increase and maintain the temperature. The cold water in the beaker will gradually warm up and will need replacing with fresh cold water.

Testing the fractions

  1. Record the colour of each fraction. Gently shake the tubes and rate the viscosity of each fraction, from 1 (least viscous or ‘runniest’) to 4 (most viscous or ‘thickest’). Rate the ease of ignition of each fraction. Pour a little of each fraction onto the watch glass and ignite it with the flame from a burning splint. Rate the fractions from 1 (most easily ignited) to 4 (least easily ignited).
  • Wear eye protection.
  • Avoid breathing the vapours formed.
  • Be careful with hot apparatus – let it cool down fully before tidying away.
  • Ensure a 0–350 °C thermometer is used for this demonstration. If a 0–100 °C thermometer is used, it will explode as the temperature increases beyond 100 °C.
  • Some students may not fully appreciate that a fraction contains different hydrocarbons with similar, but not identical, properties. Make sure students understand that a fraction is not a chemically pure substance, but is a mixture of similar substances.
