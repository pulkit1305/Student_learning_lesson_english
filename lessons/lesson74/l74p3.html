Learning objectives

Learning objectives

In this lesson, students will learn to:

  • describe how fractional distillation is used to separate crude oil into fractions
  • state the names of the fractions in order
  • explain how molecular size determines the fraction in which the molecule appears
  • describe the trends in the fractions
  • relate the uses of the fractions to their properties.

Key definitions

Key definitions

  • fuel: a substance that when burned in oxygen releases heat energy
  • refinery gases: a mixture of methane, ethane, propane and butane; this mixture is commonly used as liquefied petroleum gas (LPG) for domestic heating and cooking

Teaching session 1

Fractions

Properties of fractions

  • Each fraction is a mixture of hydrocarbons. It is not a pure substance.
  • The hydrocarbons in each fraction have similar numbers of carbon and hydrogen atoms in their molecules so they have similar boiling points.
  • The uses of a fraction are related to its properties.
  • The hydrocarbons in the refinery gases fraction are easy to ignite so they are used as fuels.
  • Bitumen is a solid at room temperature and it is waterproof so it can be used for surfacing roads and roofs.

Describe the relationship between the number of carbon atoms in a hydrocarbon molecule and the physical properties of the hydrocarbon.

Answer: The greater the number of carbon atoms; the higher the boiling point; the more difficult it is to ignite/lower ease of ignition; the higher the viscosity/the more viscous/the less easily it flows.
