Lesson 45 | Teacher guidance Concentration Calculations

  • Worksheets will need to be printed for students in advance of the lesson.
  • While all care is taken to ensure resource content is in line with cultural sensitivities, please be aware that it should be reviewed before sharing with students.

Solutions

  • Show the students a variety of different pieces of glassware with measurement scales (for example, 10 cm3 measuring cylinder, 50 cm3 measuring cylinder, 250 cm3 conical flask), each filled to the mark with aqueous sodium chloride of concentration 5 g/dm3. (You could just use tap water and tell the students that it is the sodium chloride solution.)
  • Ask students: Which container has the most concentrated solution in it? (Answer: They are all the same.)
  • If they realise they are all the same, ask students:
    • What is different about the solutions? (Answer: The volume of solution is different in each container, so the actual mass of solute in each is different [but the concentration, grams per cubic decimetre, is the same].)
    • Can you work out the mass of sodium chloride dissolved in the water in each container? (Answer: This will depend on sizes of glassware used, but for examples given above, using a solution of concentration 5 g/dm3: 10 cm3 contains 0.05 g, 50 cm3 contains 0.25 g, 250 cm3 contains 1.25 g.)
    • How could you use a practical method to check to if your answers are correct? (Answer: Students should suggest finding the mass of solution in an evaporating basin, heating the solution until all the water has evaporated, and then finding the mass of the salt left.)
  • If they think the concentrations are different, tell students:
    • They are the same solution, so the concentration is the same, but each different volume contains a different mass of solute.
    • You can calculate this: for example: 1000 cm3 of solution contains 5 g of sodium chloride, so 10 cm3 of solution contains \((\frac5{1000})\) × 10 = 0.05 g.

Concentration

  • Display Slide 1 of the slideshow.
  • Tell students:
    • You have seen that the mass of a specific number of particles can be weighed out by converting moles into mass.
    • With solutions however, measuring a volume is more useful as different solutions will have different densities.
    • A solution is made when a soluble substance, known as the solute, is dissolved in a solvent such as water.
    • When deciding how much of a solution to use in a reaction, it is important to know the concentration.
    • The concentration of a solution depends on the ratio of solvent to solute.
  • Display Slide 2 of the slideshow.
  • Tell students:
    • Molar concentration is when 1 mole of solute dissolves in 1 dm3 of solution making a solution with a concentration of 1 mol/dm3.
    • The concentration of a solution is measured as the amount of solute per unit volume of solution. The unit of volume used is the dm3. Concentration is the amount, in moles or grams, of solute in each dm3 of solution.
  • Tell students: Remember that in calculations it might be necessary to convert between various units.
  • Display Slide 3 of the slideshow.
  • Display Slide 4 of the slideshow.
  • Using mini whiteboards or paper, ask students:
    • Can you calculate these answers?
    • Remember to always double check the units of the volume you are working with. In order to calculate concentration in mol/dm3, the volume must be converted to dm3 if it is in cm3.
  • Slide 5 of the slideshow can be used to show how the answers are worked out, or you could simply use them to enable quick marking of the mini whiteboards/paper calculations. A glance around the room should give a good insight into how students are coping with this new calculation.

Support

For students who struggle with Concentration:

  • In Student work 1, ask students to complete Worksheet 1 in pairs or small groups.
  • Ensure that they have the help images at the base of the worksheet present and know how to use the converter chart or the formula triangle.

Reacting volumes

  • In this part of the lesson, a practical method known as titration is mentioned. For now, students just need to know that the technique exists (some students may have used a burette before) but it is the calculation part of the entire titration process that we are focusing on in this part of the lesson.
  • Tell students:
    • In the same way that the mass of a reactant can be used along with the molar ratio to calculate the maximum yield of a product, concentrations and volumes can be used to determine the volume required of another reactant or the concentration of an unknown solution.
    • A practical method that you will learn more about in future is a titration. For now, we shall look at the mathematical theory rather than the practical technique.
  • Display Slides 1–4 of the slideshow.
  • The process is very similar to the one that was used when determining reacting masses. Work through each slide in turn, explaining what is being calculated at each stage. Students could write down the worked example, annotating what is being done at each stage.
  • Display Slide 5 of the slideshow.
  • Tell students: In the exam, it is possible you will be provided with different pieces of information from those presented here. You should be able to rearrange formulae and convert units to find the value requested in the question.
  • Work through the final example prior to students carrying out Student work 2.

Support

For students who struggle with Reacting volumes:

  • Next, they should look at the balanced equation for the molar ratio to determine the moles of ethanoic acid.
  • Finally, they should use the moles of ethanoic acid and volume of ethanoic acid given in the question to calculate the concentration of ethanoic acid. Q2 follows a similar step-by-step process.
  • Give students Worksheet 3.

Recap question

  • Display the Exam-style question.
  • Tell students: You have 3 minutes to complete the exam-style question and write your answer in your exercise book.
  • Leave the question displayed on the board during this time, and display a digital timer on the board so students can see how long they have left.
  • After the 3 minutes have elapsed, ask students to pair up with their partner to confer about what they have done before you reveal the answer step-by-step.
  • Allow time for students to mark their work. They should assign marks where they are shown. Ask students to raise their hands to show how many marks they obtained.
  • Hand out the Homework sheet. For most students, remove the equations from the bottom of the sheet before photocopying or printing.
  • Q1 covers calculating concentration from mass and volume.
  • Q2 covers calculating mass from concentration and volume.
  • Q3 covers calculating concentration from moles and volume.
  • Q4 covers calculating moles from concentration and volume.
  • Q5 covers converting concentration from g/dm3 to mol/dm3.
  • Q6 covers converting concentration from mol/dm3 to g/dm3.

Reflection

  • Ask students the following three questions:
    • State one thing you have learnt.
    • State one thing you are still unsure about.
    • State one thing you would like to improve next lesson.
  • Ask students to write their answers on paper or in their exercise book. Take a sample of statements from the class from students who are willing to discuss their answers. You can check any other statements by looking at the papers or books. If there is any striking issue, this should be addressed at the start of the next lesson.
