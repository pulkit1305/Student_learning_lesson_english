Learning objectives

Learning objectives

In this lesson, students will learn to:

  • explain what has to happen for reactions to take place
  • explain why changes in the frequency of collisions between particles affect the rate of reaction
  • describe how to calculate rate of reaction from experimental data.

Key definitions

Key definitions

  • activation energy: the minimum amount of energy required for a collision to be successful, i.e. result in a reaction
  • collision theory: states that for a reaction to occur, the reactant particles must collide with each other, in the correct orientation and with sufficient energy
  • concentration: the amount of solute dissolved in a certain volume of solvent; in general, if you increase the concentration of reactants in a reaction, the rate of reaction increases
  • rate: the speed at which the amount of reactant decreases or the amount of product increases; it is measured as the change in concentration of reactants or products per unit time
  • surface area: the area on the surface of a solid that is exposed

Teaching session 1

Measuring rates of reactions

Activation energy

When particles collide, they must do so with a certain amount of energy to cause a reaction.
The minimum amount of energy required to result in a reaction is called the activation energy, and collisions that happen with an energy equal to or above the activation energy will result in a reaction.

Orientation

Molecules need to collide with certain parts of other molecules to cause a reaction.
Sometimes, in big compounds, large groups of atoms will block the attacking species from the area of the molecule that it is attacking. This happens if it approaches from the wrong direction.

Successful collisions

A successful collision is one that results in a reaction.

A successful collision is one that is in the correct orientation and has energy equal to or greater than the activation energy.

To increase the rate of a reaction, successful collisions need to happen more often. This is referred to as increasing the frequency of successful collisions.
