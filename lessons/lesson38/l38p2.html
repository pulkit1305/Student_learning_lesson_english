Lesson 38 | Teacher guidance Rates of Reaction: Surface Area and Concentration

  • Worksheets will need to be printed for students in advance of the lesson.
  • While all care is taken to ensure resource content is in line with cultural sensitivities, please be aware that it should be reviewed before sharing with students.

Reaction speeds

  • Ask students to work in pairs to write down as many chemical reactions as they can think of, classifying them under three headings:
    • Very slow reactions (e.g., rusting)
    • Medium speed reactions (e.g., cooking)
    • Very fast reactions (e.g., explosions and precipitations).
  • Go through students' ideas, picking pairs at random.
  • Then ask students: Why might we want to speed reactions up or slow them down?
    • Answers may include the following:
      • increasing the speed of reactions that produce useful products (e.g., the Haber process) increases profits
      • increasing the speed of reactions that remove harmful substances (e.g., in a catalytic converter) allows harmful substances to be removed more effectively
      • decreasing the speed of reactions that cause problems (e.g., rusting) helps minimise the problems.
  • Some students may need examples of chemical reactions that have different rates. Consider giving less able students a list of reactions to classify. Some examples could include: combustion of a piece of wood, oxidation happening when metal rusts, oxidation happening in a firework, combustion of petrol in a car engine, oxidation when someone strikes a match.
  • Challenge more able students who have listed any reactions that they have met in the course to write balanced equations for those reactions.

Measuring rates of reactions

  • This lesson considers the changes that occur to reactants and products during chemical reactions. Practical methods for measuring reaction rates are considered and graphs are used to present experimental data. You should encourage students to examine the graphs, note axes and units and identify trends.
  • Tell students: When you walk you are moving at a low speed, but when you run you are moving at a high speed. Your speed is not how far you go, but how quickly. The rate of a reaction is the rate at which a reaction proceeds – not how much product is made, but how quickly it is made. It is possible to measure the rate of reaction in two ways:
    • by measuring the rate of a product formed, or
    • by measuring the rate of a reactant used up.
  • Ask students: What happens to the concentrations of reactants and products during a reaction? (Answer: The concentration of reactants decreases and the concentration of products increases.)
  • Ask students: Why do most reactions slow down in time? (Answer: Because the concentration of reactants decreases as the reaction proceeds.)
  • Use a tray of marbles to revise ideas about solids, liquids and gases and collisions of particles, and discuss why two solids rarely react together.
  • Demonstrate the idea that collisions are needed for reactions to occur, using a tray with a solid block of wood in the centre. Then add some marbles to the tray and ask what has to happen for them to react with the solid lump. Shake the tray and show that moving molecules hit the solid.
  • Ask: How might the reaction rate be increased? (Answer: Shake the tray more; break up the piece of wood into smaller pieces; use more marbles.) Explain that the reactant particles must collide with each other in the correct orientation and with sufficient energy.
  • Display Slide 1 of the Slideshow: Measuring rates of reactions and explain about activation energy.
  • You could use a Bunsen burner to make the point in this demonstration. Ask students: Why does a Bunsen burner not light as soon as you turn on the gas? (Answer: Because the molecules do not have enough energy [the activation energy].)
  • Display Slide 2 of the slideshow.
  • Explain that by combining all this information together we can state that a successful collision is one that results in a reaction.
  • Tell students: Write a definition for a successful collision.
    • A typical definition would be: A successful collision is one that is in the correct orientation and has energy equal to or greater than the activation energy.
    • This is known as the collision theory.
    • Ask students to check their definition against this one; select some students to read their version. They could add diagrams to their notes.
  • It is important to get this section firmly established because the next part of this lesson and other subsequent lessons will refer to successful conditions.
  • Display Slide 3 of the slideshow.
  • Tell students: To increase the frequency of successful collisions, particles either need to collide more often, or a higher proportion of the collisions that occur need to be successful, i.e., have enough energy to result in a reaction. The slide shows ways of increasing frequency and success.

Measuring rates of reaction

  • In this part of the lesson, practical methods for measuring reaction rates are considered. The presentation consolidates the material covered in the IG Chemistry Student Book and includes some work on interpreting graphs.
  • Go through the slides in the presentation. It will help the students if you can show the actual pieces of equipment as they are mentioned, rather than just using the images. At this stage there is no need to go into detail about the experiments because students will be completing some of these in their next few lessons.
  • Tell students: In the next few lessons, you will see some experiments designed to determine the effect of changing some variables on the rate of a reaction. The rate of a reaction is the speed at which reactants are turned into products. Rates can be very slow (rusting) to almost instantaneous (explosions).
  • Display Slide 1 of the Slideshow: Measuring and recording rates of reaction.
  • Tell students: To investigate reaction rates we need to be able to measure how the amount of reactants or products changes with time. Look at the graph.
  • Ask students:
    • What does the slope of the graph indicate? (Answer: The steeper the slope/gradient the faster the reaction.)
    • Tell students: Remember that, during a reaction, the concentration of reactants decreases, which causes the reaction to slow down over time.
    • Apart from concentration, what other ways could we monitor reaction rates? (Answer: Measure changes in mass or volume of reactants or products; more difficult but could also measure changes in colour/turbidity.)
    • What piece(s) of apparatus can be used to measure gas volumes accurately? (Answer: A gas syringe, a measuring cylinder or even a top pan balance.)
  • Display Slide 2 of the slideshow.
  • Tell students: The upturned measuring cylinder needs to be filled with water so that the volume of gas produced can be seen. In the picture on the right, we can follow the rate by measuring the change in mass. The mass decreases as carbon dioxide escapes from the flask.
  • Ask students: Can you explain why measuring the volume of gas produced can be used to monitor the rates of some reactions? (Answer: In a reaction that produces a gas, measuring the volume of gas produced relates to the amount of product formed and so it can be used to measure how far the reaction has gone in a certain time interval.)
  • Display Slides 3–6 of the slideshow.
  • Tell students: A graph can be used to determine the rate of a reaction at any point. For the purposes of this explanation, a graph showing concentration of one of the reactants has been selected.
  • Tell students: Determining the gradient of a tangent is done exactly the same way that it is done in maths:

Alternative activities

  • These resources can be used if you are splitting the content of this lesson into two sessions.

What do you know?

  • Ask students: What is meant by ‘rate of reaction’? How can we speed up or slow down the rate of a reaction? (Answer: The rate of a reaction is the speed at which reactants are turned into products; rates vary greatly from explosions [almost instantaneous] to rusting [very slow and gradual]; rates of reactions can be controlled by altering variables: increasing or decreasing temperature, concentration, or surface area or by adding a catalyst.)
  • In pairs, give students 5 minutes to discuss, draw and write down some ideas.
  • Each pair should share their answers with another pair and amend or add to their answers as necessary. Groups can then feed back one or more of their answers to the class.
  • Collate answers given into a class mind map on the board. This could be captured digitally and saved to display on the board at the end of the lesson. (Note: Do not correct any errors at this stage, the mind map will be re-visited in the Alternative end-of-lesson activity.)
