Reversible reactions and dynamic equilibrium quiz

  • Students have now completed several lessons on reversible reactions and dynamic equilibrium. The short quiz should help them to recall some of the key words and terms from the last few lessons.
  • Provide students with mini whiteboards and display Slide 1 of the Slideshow: Reversible reactions and dynamic equilibrium quiz, which features the key words for the quiz.
  • Tell students: I will now read out some definitions and on your whiteboard, you should write the relevant key word from those shown on the slide. Show your whiteboard after the count of three each time.
    • with water (Answer: Hydrated)
    • without water (Answer: Anhydrous)
    • when the forward and backward reaction in a reversible chemical reaction occur at the same rate (Answer: Dynamic equilibrium)
    • when substances cannot enter or leave an observed environment, such as a stoppered test tube (Answer: Closed system)
    • A chemical reaction that can work in both directions. (Answer: reversible reaction.)
    • A system into or from which substances can enter or leave, such as a reaction inside an unstoppered test tube. (Answer: Open system)
  • Briefly discuss any incorrect answers which have been produced by students to help correct any misconceptions.

Position of equilibrium

  • Tell students: In the previous lesson, you learned that when a reaction reaches equilibrium the proportions of reactants and products present in the reaction mixture are not necessarily in equal amounts.
  • Display Slide 1 of the slideshow.
  • Tell students: Write down the example reaction shown. Then deconstruct the equation and write down the forward reaction and the reverse reaction.
  • Take a selection of answers before displaying the answers on Slide 2.
  • Display Slide 3.
  • Tell students: Using the previous example, when the reaction reaches equilibrium, it is possible to measure the proportions of the reactants and the products.
  • Ask students:
    • Can you determine the reactants and products in the mixture at equilibrium? (Answer: 75% of the reaction mixture is the reactants and 25% is the products.)
    • Where does the position of equilibrium lie? (Answer: There are more reactants [A + B] than products [C + D]. This means that the position of equilibrium lies to the left.)
  • Tell students: Don’t be confused about the numbers in front of C and D. These do not have an impact on the proportions at equilibrium.
  • Display Slide 4.
  • Tell students: Using the new example, when the reaction reaches equilibrium, it is possible to measure the proportions of the reactants and the products.
  • Ask students:
    • Can you determine the reactants and products in the mixture at equilibrium? (Answer: 10% of the reaction mixture is the reactants and 90% is the products.)
    • Where does the position of equilibrium lie? (Answer: There are more products [Y + Z] than reactants [W + X]. This means that the position of equilibrium lies to the right.)
  • Tell students: The concentrations or proportions of reactants and products at equilibrium will remain constant until the conditions surrounding the reaction change. Later in this lesson we will look at how changing certain reaction conditions can shift the position of equilibrium in a reversible reaction.

Effect of changing conditions on the position of equilibrium

  • Tell students:
    • In a closed system where dynamic equilibrium is reached, it is possible to alter some conditions causing the position of equilibrium to shift.
    • Chemists will normally try to shift the position of equilibrium to the right ⁠– this is done to make more products.
    • Factors that can be changed include pressure, concentration, and temperature. Concentration is not needed for the Edexcel International GCSE (9–1) Chemistry examination and so will not be looked at in any more detail.
    • Particles in solids and liquids are not affected by changes in pressure, so this change only affects gases.
    • There is a general rule which states that the position of equilibrium shifts to counteract the effect of any changes to the system.
  • Display Slide 1 of the Slideshow: Effect of changing conditions on the position of equilibrium .
  • Tell students:
    • Changing the temperature of a reaction can be done to alter the rate, but at the same time this might reduce the yield.
    • For example, if the backward reaction was endothermic then an increase of temperature to speed up the rate would force the reaction to shift the position of equilibrium to the left.
  • Display Slide 2.
  • Tell students: Altering pressure only affects reactions in which gases are present.
  • Tell students:
    • You will see in a later lesson that adding a catalyst has no effect on the position of equilibrium but will increase the rate at which equilibrium is reached.
    • In exam questions, you will need to fully analyse conditions. You cannot assume that the conditions provided shift the position of equilibrium to the right to increase the yield, even though that is normally what chemists want to happen.

Alternative activities

  • These resources can be used if you are splitting the content of this lesson into two sessions.

    Quick quiz

    • Give students mini whiteboards.
    • Ask students: Write your answer down for each of the following questions:
      1. How can you increase the energy or speed of the particles of a substance? (Answer: By increasing the temperature.)
      2. What change to gas pressure could increase the rate of a reaction? (Answer: Increasing the pressure.)
      3. What happens to the position of equilibrium in a reaction if the temperature is increased? (Answer: The equilibrium position shifts in the direction of the endothermic reaction.)
      4. What effect does adding a catalyst have on the position of equilibrium? (Answer: It has no effect on the position of the equilibrium [but does increase the rate at which equilibrium is reached].)
    • Ask students to hold up their answer after a count of three before you tell them the correct answer. This will give a good overview of the class understanding. If they are not sure or do not have an answer, they should draw a question mark ‘?’ so that all students are made to respond. Try and address any issues that come up as they arise.

Confidence rating
