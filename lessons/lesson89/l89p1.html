Lesson 89 | Overview Changes in Position of Equilibrium

3.22C
know that a reversible reaction can reach dynamic equilibrium in a sealed container
  • an increase (or decrease) in temperature shifts the position of equilibrium in the direction of the endothermic (or exothermic) reaction
  • an increase (or decrease) in pressure shifts the position of equilibrium in the direction that produces fewer (or more) moles of gas (references to Le Chatelier's principle are not required)

In this lesson, students will learn to:

  • explain the term position of equilibrium
  • explain how a change in temperature will affect the position of equilibrium
  • explain how a change in pressure may affect the position of equilibrium
  • explain the change in position of equilibrium, if any, as temperature and pressure are altered.

Students should be familiar with dynamic equilibrium from their work in Lesson 88.

  • position of equilibrium: a reference to the proportion of the various things in an equilibrium mixture; if the position of equilibrium of a reaction lies towards the right, the equilibrium mixture contains a higher proportion of products than reactants
  • pressure: the force acting on something per unit area; pressure has the SI unit of Pa (N/m2) and is caused by molecules hitting the wall of their container

Pearson Edexcel International GCSE (9–1) Chemistry Student Book: pp. 242–246


  • There are no practicals in this lesson.
  • Some students may struggle with the concept of position of equilibrium and proportions of reactants and products. Many assume that at equilibrium the proportions of reactants and products are equal. You will need to remind students that equilibrium does not mean that an equal mixture of reactants and products are present. It is the point in a reversible reaction when the proportions or concentrations of the reactants and products are not changing. The amounts are not equal but they are constant.
