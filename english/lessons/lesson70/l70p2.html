Lesson 70 | Teacher guidance Narrative Voice

  • While all care is taken to ensure resource content is in line with cultural sensitivities, please be aware that it should be reviewed before sharing with students.

The differences between first and third person

  • Display Slide 1 of the slideshow.
  • Ask students to identify and note the differences between these two texts. 
  • Take feedback to share ideas. Listen for responses that recognise that:
    • Extract A is written in the first person, using the pronouns ‘I’, ‘me’ and ‘my’.
    • Extract is written in the third person, using the pronouns ‘he’, ‘him’ and ‘his’.
  • Point out that in the simple past tense (in which both extracts are written), the verb forms themselves do not change.
  • Display Slide 2, on which both extracts are written in the present tense.
  • Ask students to identify the differences in verb form in the two versions. 
  • Answers:
    • I hear/he hears.
    • I clench/he clenches.
    • I know/he knows.
  • Note that other verb forms in the two extracts do not change (for example, ‘crackles’) because the verb’s subject (‘a shiver of terror’) is not affected by the change from first to third person.
  • Emphasise that verb forms can change to ‘agree’ with their subjects, depending on the tense used.

The impact of third and first person

  • Display Slide 2 of the slideshow from the Starter.
  • Ask students to identify the writer’s intentions in the given extracts. Listen for responses that show awareness of the intention to:
    • create a sense of fear
    • create tension.
  • Ask students: Which of the two versions do you feel achieves these intentions most effectively?
  • Give students a minute to consider and/or discuss with a partner which version they feel is more effective and why.
  • Take feedback. Validate any relevant responses. Aim to draw out awareness that:
    • when a narrative focuses on thoughts and feelings, the use of the first person creates a greater sense of connection between the reader and the narrator, while in the third person the character is more distanced or detached from the reader
    • this is particularly noticeable when a character’s thoughts or feelings are the focus of the narrative.

Task 1: Writing in the third or first person (5 minutes)

  • Give students a couple of minutes to write two to four sentences, of up to a total of around forty words, in which they describe the thoughts and feelings of a character facing something frightening. It could be:
    • a frightening person, for example a burglar in the house
    • a frightening animal, for example a tiger
    • a frightening situation, for example having to make a speech in front of a huge audience.
  • Ask students to raise a hand if they wrote their sentences in the third person, and again if they wrote them in the first person.
  • Invite volunteers to explain the reasons for their choice.

Task 2: Transforming the narrative voice (10 minutes)

  • Give students five minutes to re-write their sentences, transforming it from either a first-person narrative to a third-person narrative, or vice versa.
  • Ask students to swap their writing with a partner and check the accuracy of their partner’s transformation, focusing on the use of pronouns and verb forms.
  • Ask students: Which of the two versions of your writing do you think is more effective?
  • Invite volunteers to explain their choice.
  • Point out to students that, while a first-person narrative can be very effective in creating a connection with the reader, it can be limiting: for example, everything that happens in the story has to be seen or experienced by the narrator, or recounted to the narrator by another character in the story.

Support

For students who struggle with the impact of third and first person:

  • Support students’ preparation for writing in Student work 1 by working as a class to note the symptoms of fear, for example: heart racing, palms sweating, stomach churning, fists clenching, and so on.
  • Support students in transforming their writing from first to third person (or vice versa) by first asking them to scan their writing and underline every pronoun and verb, before adapting them as necessary.

Analysing a narrative voice

  • Explain to students: We are going to read a short extract from a novel and explore how the writer creates the narrator’s voice.
  • Read the extract from The Salt Road on pp. 218–19 of the Student Book.
  • As a whole class, discuss each of the bullet-pointed question prompts above the extract on p. 218: questions to ask when considering the narrative voice in any prose fiction. Listen for responses that recognise the following.
    • The story is told from one character’s first-person point of view, creating the mood and impression of an intimate autobiography.
    • The narrator’s parents live in ‘museum-like calm’ while the narrator is a ‘wild little creature’.
    • The narrator resents or fears her parents’ bitterness and their apparent lack of interest in her. 
    • The narrator has a vivid imagination, and prefers playing with boys, having little interest in dolls other than when she ‘beheaded or scalped them’.
    • There is no evidence to suggest the reader should not trust the narrator’s point of view although, as a child, she may be misinterpreting her parents’ lives and attitudes.
  • Invite volunteers to suggest words and phrases to describe the character of the narrator and her voice. Note them on the board, under the heading ‘narrator’. (Possible answers: imaginative, wild, resentful, bitter, destructive)
  • Invite volunteers to suggest words and phrases to describe the narrator’s parents. Note them on the board, under the heading ‘parents’. (Possible answers: quiet, organised, isolated, uninterested in their daughter)
  • Ask students to complete Activity 2 on p. 219 of the Student Book.
  • Once they are finished, ask them to swap their writing with a partner.
  • Invite students to share any particularly effective sentences or paragraphs from their partner’s writing with the class.

Support

For students who struggle with analysing a narrative voice:

  • Encourage students to focus on the contrast the writer creates between the ideas and attitudes of the narrator, and the ideas and attitudes of her parents. Ask: What effect does this contrast have? Listen for responses that recognise that the strong contrast emphasises the difference between the narrator and her parents: it exaggerates the narrator’s wildness and her parents’ quietness.
  • Encourage students, in their response to Activity 2, to analyse the impact of the writer’s choice of vocabulary, sentence structure and first-person narration in creating the reader’s response to the narrator’s home life. For example:
    • The choice of first person engages the reader in the narrator’s point of view and her attitudes to her parents.
    • The adjective ‘museum-like’ has connotations of organisation and education, suggesting the calm and quiet of her parents’ house. This contrasts with the narrator’s description of herself as a ‘wild little creature’ suggesting she is like an untamed animal.
    • The writer builds vivid description using lists. She lists the contents of the parents’ house (‘boxes of yellowed papers, ancient artefacts, dusty objects…'). Similarly she lists the qualities of the narrator: ‘wild little creature, loud and messy and unbiddable.’

Additional activities

  • If splitting the content of this lesson into two shorter sessions, these additional activities can be used to begin and end the sessions.

First and third person

  • If splitting the lesson to focus on the impact of third and first person in the first session, and analysing a narrative voice in the second, this activity can be used to begin the second session.
  • Ask students to note as many of the key differences between the first and third person as they can remember from the first session. 
  • Take feedback.
  • Answers:
    • The use of pronouns I/me/my in the first person and he/him/his, she/her/hers, they/them/their in the third person
    • The agreement of verb and subject
    • The closer bond that first person can create between the reader and main character/narrator.
  • If splitting the lesson to focus on the impact of third and first person in the first session, and analysing a narrative voice in the second, this activity can be used to conclude the first session.
  • Remind students that one of the limitations of a story told in the first person is that the narrator must be involved in some way in everything that happens in the story: they must appear in every ‘scene’.
  • Give students two minutes to discuss with a partner: What kinds of story would be better told in the third person?
  • Prompt students to consider stories they have read that were told in the third person. Why do they think the writer chose to do that?
  • Responses could include the following.
    • It allows the writer to develop a range of characters more fully instead of concentrating on just one.
    • It allows the writer to show different characters in different places at the same point in time – a feature common in, for example, crime and adventure stories.
