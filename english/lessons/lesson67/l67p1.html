Lesson 67 | Overview Reading a Poem

AO1
Read and understand a variety of texts, selecting and interpreting information, ideas and perspectives.
AO2
Understand and analyse how writers use linguistic and structural devices to achieve their effects.

In this lesson, students will learn to:

  • explore three poems, considering the writer’s intentions and choices
  • explore the impact of figurative language.

Students should already be familiar with:

  • identifying the writer’s intentions
  • analysing the writer’s choices
  • figurative language: simile, metaphor and personification.
  • figurative language: a language device creating a comparison to convey a vivid image, most commonly through simile, metaphor or personification
  • metaphor: describing something by comparing it to an image that it resembles, in a way that says the object is the image
  • personification: a type of metaphor in which human qualities or actions are attributed to something non-human
  • simile: a description that says that an object is like an image
  • flame tree: a species of tree found widely in the Caribbean
  • crab’s leg, fried plantain: typical Caribbean food
  • kissogram: a humorous greeting

Pearson Edexcel International GCSE (9–1) English Language A Student Book: pp. 208–12


  • Sometimes, some students may consider a poem to be like a puzzle or a code to be solved, with only one correct ‘answer’. Encourage students to develop their own personal response to each text they encounter, making clear that any response that is relevant and supported with evidence from the text can be considered valid.
