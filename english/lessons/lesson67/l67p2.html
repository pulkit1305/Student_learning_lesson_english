Lesson 67 | Teacher guidance Reading a Poem

  • While all care is taken to ensure resource content is in line with cultural sensitivities, please be aware that it should be reviewed before sharing with students.

What is it?

  • Explain to students: In the next series of lessons, we are going to be exploring prose and poetry: fiction texts and poems. In Section A of Paper 2 of your exam, you will be asked to write an analysis of one of the poems or prose texts from Part 2 of your Anthology: Paper 2, Section A, Poetry and Prose texts.
  • Display Slide 1 of the slideshow.
  • Ask students to identify what they can see on the slide.
  • Listen for responses that recognise the text as an extract from a poem.
  • Ask volunteers to identify the clues that suggest this.
  • Possible answers:
    • The form, set out in short lines
    • The use of imagery/metaphor and careful choice of language
    • The initially indistinct meaning.
  • Display Slide 2.
  • Point out to students that these are key features of poetry and that they will explore these key features of form, language and meaning in the five poems from the Anthology in this series of lessons.

Exploring a poem

  • Explain to students that, in this lesson and the subsequent five lessons, they will be exploring a range of poetry and prose texts. We will consider the key features of poetry and prose and their impact before concentrating on the 10 texts in the Anthology – one of which will appear on Paper 2 of your exam.
  • Focus students’ attention on the poem ‘Praise Song for my Mother’ on pp. 208–09 of the Student Book.
  • Read the poem aloud.
  • Invite volunteers to respond to the poem by expressing a positive or negative response about its form, language or meaning. Use the three prompts shown on Slide 2 of the slideshow in the Starter to encourage responses.
  • Aim to develop students’ responses with secondary questioning, for example:
    • Why do you think that?
    • What effect does that feature have?
    • How has the poet done that?
    • Why do you think the poet has done that?
  • Point out that responding to a poem is very different from ‘decoding’ a poem. The latter is simply a case of identifying what the poet means; the former is exploring how the poet has expressed their ideas and how the reader responds to them. Point out that each reader’s response to the key elements of a poem may be different.
  • Explain to students that they are going to develop their response to the poem by answering a series of questions that will help them explore the poem’s ideas and how the poet has conveyed them.
  • Ask students to complete Activity 1 on p. 209 of the Student Book. You may want students to discuss and note their responses in pairs.
  • Take feedback to share ideas.
  • Possible answers:
    • Q1. A daughter who respected, and is grateful to, her mother.
    • Q2. The speaker’s late mother who had a significant influence and impact on her daughter’s life.
    • Q3/4/5. All responses are valid. Students may focus on a specific image, for example: the image of ‘water’, suggesting life and sustenance; ‘sunrise’ suggesting warmth, life, newness; images of Caribbean food and the Caribbean environment (flame tree, fried plantain) suggesting the importance of the heritage and sustenance her mother gave her.
    • Q6. To express all that her mother did for her, and her gratitude for it.
    • Q7. All responses are valid if relevant and supported with reference to the text.

Support

For students who struggle with exploring a poem:

  • Work with students as a whole class,or in a group,on questions 1–6 of Activity 1 on p. 209 to ensure students’ understanding and encourage their confidence in that understanding.
  • For questions 1, 2 and 6, focus students' attention on the title of the poem. What does this suggest about the speaker, her subject and her intention? Having deduced this, ask students to find further evidence to support their ideas in the body of the poem.
  • For questions 3–5, encourage students to express an honest, personal response, encouraging them to justify it.
  • Note relevant responses on the board for students’ reference in their independent response to question 7.

Exploring figurative language

  • Introduce the term ‘figurative language’: language devices that make comparisons to make images and descriptions more vivid.
  • Explain to students: The three most common types of figurative language, which you are likely to encounter in a text, are simile, metaphor and personification.
  • To check students' understanding of simile, metaphor and personification, focus their attention on Activity 1 on p. 210 of the Student Book.
  • Ask students to consider and note similes, metaphors or personification for any or all of the given images using the prompts in the activity. Point out that definitions of each are given in the vocabulary box on p. 210.
  • Take feedback to share ideas and ensure appropriateness of choices.
  • Possible answers:
    • The flames were as a hot as the sun.
    • The mushroom looked like a thin man in a wide hat.
    • The beach was a playground.
    • The mountain is a giant.
    • The flames danced.
    • The sea whispered.
  • Display the slideshow.
  • Explain to students: We are going to read two poems. In one of them, the writer compares love to an onion. One way to think about simile, metaphor and personification is to imagine a diagram like the one on the slide. 
  • Ask students: What points of similarity can you think of between love and an onion?
  • Note students’ responses in the central overlapping region of the Venn diagram on the displayed slide. 
  • Possible answers:
    • It has many layers.
    • It is strong and overpowering.
    • It can make you cry.
  • Explain to students: Poetry can use figurative language in surprising ways to make you think differently about the world.
  • Read the poems on p. 211 of the Student Book aloud: ‘Love is…’ by Adrian Henri and ‘Valentine’ by Carol Ann Duffy.
  • Ask students to discuss and complete Activity 2, questions 1 and 2 on p.212 of the Student Book, working with a partner.
  • Take feedback to share ideas. 
  • Possible answers:
    • Q1. ‘Valentine’:
      • Comparing an onion to the moon suggests romance. 
      • The poet compares love with an onion, suggesting it will make you cry, its fierce ‘kiss’ is overpowering but also suggests faithfulness; and, disturbingly, its ‘scent will cling to your fingers,/cling to your knife.’
    • Q1. ‘Love is…’:
      • The various comparisons suggest images of comfort, adoration, happiness, sensuality and so on.
    • Q2. ‘Valentine’ presents a far more negative, though arguably honest, view of love than the almost relentlessly romantic views of ‘Love is…’.

Support

For students who struggle with exploring figurative language:

  • In Activity 2, focus students initially on identifying comparisons in both poems that appeal to them.
  • Looking carefully at each image selected, ask students: Does this image present a positive or a negative view of love? Why?
  • Ask students to note their ideas and use them in response to Activity 2, questions 1 and 2.
  • Encourage students to consider how the poets present love through their use of metaphor: the cumulative effect of the metaphors each poet uses.
  • Students can sum up their ideas by completing the following sentences with a list of appropriate adjectives:
    • Duffy presents love as… (Possible answers: powerful, overwhelming, dangerous, threatening)
    • Henri presents love as… (Possible answers: comforting, exciting, sensual)
  • Focus students on Activity 2, question 3 on p. 212 of the Student Book.
  • Read the task and given example.
  • Ask students: In what ways can life be compared to a rollercoaster?
  • Give students two minutes to discuss and note ideas.
  • Take feedback, encouraging students to add any effective ideas to their notes in readiness for the homework task.
  • Possible answers:
    • Sometimes exciting, sometimes terrifying
    • Anticipation and disappointment at times
    • A journey on a fixed course
    • A journey with friends and strangers.
  • Distribute the homework sheet.
  • Ask students to complete Activity 2, question 3, also available on the homework sheet.
  • Emphasise that students can either develop the metaphor of the rollercoaster throughout the entire poem (as Carol Ann Duffy did with the metaphor of an onion in ‘Valentine’) or think of lots of metaphors for life (as Adrian Henri did with metaphors for love in ‘Love is…’).

Additional activities

  • If splitting the content of this lesson into two shorter sessions, these additional activities can be used to begin and end the sessions.
  • If splitting the lesson to focus on exploring a poem in the first session, and exploring figurative language in the second, this activity can be used to begin the second session.
  • Display Slide 1 of the slideshow.
  • Ask students to identify which example shows a metaphor and which shows a simile. (Answer: The original poem extract uses a metaphor. The addition of ‘like’ converts it to a simile.)
  • Display Slide 2.
  • Ask students to complete the tasks, either independently or as a whole class.
  • Possible answers:
    • She was as fierce as a lion./She was a lion.
    • Her eyes were like diamonds./Her eyes were diamonds.
    • He ran at me like a charging rhinoceros./He was a charging rhinoceros.
  • Point out that the similes created attribute one aspect of the compared item to the person, whereas the use of metaphor attributes all aspects of it to that person
  • If splitting the lesson to focus on exploring a poem in the first session, and exploring figurative language in the second, this activity can be used to conclude the first session.
  • Ask students: In what ways is a poem different from narrative prose, – a short story, for example?
  • Give students two minutes to discuss and note their ideas.
  • Take feedback to share ideas.
  • Possible answers:
    • The brevity of poetry, condensing meaning and ideas
    • The use of form in poetry (rhyme, rhythm, and so on)
    • The more intense focus on the impact of language in poetry
    • The use of imagery in poetry
    • Poems do not always tell a story but can explore ideas or emotions or moments in time
    • Poems have the ability to prompt readers to re-consider their ideas or perceptions.
