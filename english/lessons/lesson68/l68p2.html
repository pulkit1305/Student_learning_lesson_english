Lesson 68 | Teacher guidance Pathetic Fallacy

  • While all care is taken to ensure resource content is in line with cultural sensitivities, please be aware that it should be reviewed before sharing with students.

Pathetic fallacy

  • Display the slideshow.
  • Ask students to select one setting and one weather-type and suggest the kind of story that might feature these details. Listen for answers reflecting the setting and weather, for example:
    • a romantic story on a beach in hot sunny weather
    • a ghostly or mysterious story in a large old house in stormy weather
    • a story of danger and survival in a tent during heavy snow.
  • In each suggestion, ask students: What would be the impact of setting that kind of story in one of the other places shown on the slide, with one of the other types of weather? Listen for (and use your comments to highlight) responses that suggest awareness of the ways in which setting can create mood or atmosphere and enhance the story’s action.
  • Reinforce students’ understanding by introducing the term ‘pathetic fallacy’: a type of figurative language in which the setting of a story reflects and enhances the mood and action of a story.

Figurative language in prose

  • Explain to students: We are going to read an extract in which the writer uses pathetic fallacy.
  • Give students a brief introduction to the extract: This is from the first chapter of Lord of the Flies. A group of schoolboys were on a plane being evacuated to safety as Britain is at war. The plane has crashed on a remote island. In this extract, Ralph is exploring the island.
  • Read the extract on p. 212 of the Student Book aloud.
  • Clarify the characters’ situation to students: A group of boys are on a desert island with sand and warm sea and no adults.
  • Ask students: Would you like to be in this situation? Ask students to note one or two reasons why they would, and one or two reasons why they would not.
  • Take feedback. Possible answers:
    • Positive: freedom, adventure, appealing environment
    • Negative: danger, isolation, unappealing environment.
  • Explain to students: You are going to explore how the writer has created the responses you have just given me.
  • Ask students to note their responses to the two bullet pointed prompts in question 1 of Activity 3 on p. 213 of the Student Book.
  • Take feedback to share ideas and understanding. Possible answers:
    • Images of decay and destruction: ‘broken trunk… coarse grass, torn… fallen trees… decaying coconuts…’
    • Ominous or unsettling details: ‘the darkness of the forest… a shadowy green and purple… always, almost visible was the heat’
    • An idyllic, stereotypical paradise: ‘fringed with palm trees… shimmering water… white surf… coral reef… blue of all the shades’).

Support

For students who struggle with figurative language in prose:

  • Write two headings on the board – ‘positive’ and ‘negative’ – reflecting the discussion of positive and negative aspects of being stranded on a remote island at the end of Teaching session 1.
  • Ask students to identify features of setting and place them under one of the headings. What does each one suggest? Ask students to select two and note their ideas.

A sense of place

  • Focus students’ attention on Activity 4 on p. 213 of the Student Book.
  • Display the slideshow.
  • Remind students of the settings given in the Starter: a large, remote house; a tent on a hill; a beach.
  • Ask students to choose the place in which they will set their description. They could use one of the ideas from the Starter or use their own idea.
  • Direct students’ attention to the moods listed on the displayed slide. Ask students to select the mood they will aim to create in their description. They could choose one of the suggestions or one of their own ideas.
  • Take feedback to share ideas and the reasons for their choices.
  • Ask students to note all the details they could use to convey a vivid image of their chosen place. Suggest that students refer to the ideas in the ‘details’ section of the displayed slide.
  • Take feedback to share ideas.
  • Direct students’ attention to the list of adjectives displayed on the slide, pointing out that all of them are taken from the extract from Lord of the Flies and are used to create the mood or atmosphere of the island.
  • Ask students to add adjectives or descriptive phrases to each of the details they have noted to help convey their chosen mood and atmosphere.
  • Take feedback to share ideas.
  • Distribute Worksheet 1.
  • Point out to students that the extract from Lord of the Flies shows a character walking through the setting, describing what he sees.
  • Suggest that students give structure to their writing by using the same technique as William Golding in the extract from Lord of the Flies: describing their chosen place from the point of view of a character walking through it or sitting or standing in it.
  • Emphasise to students that they should focus on the clarity and quality of their description, not on completing it.
  • Advise students that they will be completing the task for homework and should aim to write 150–200 words in total.
  • Ask students to begin writing their description. Students can write their description on the worksheet.

Support

For students who struggle with a sense of place:

  • Before students begin writing, ensure they have fully gathered the raw materials for their description: the mood they intend to create, the features for description and the adjectives they will use to describe those features.
  • Encourage students to imagine their description as a scene from a film, the camera following a character walking through this place and showing images of all the features that create the desired mood. Emphasise that the aim of the descriptive language in their writing is to create those images in the reader’s mind.
  • Encourage students to consider their language choices carefully: the connotations of their choice of adjectives, the (sparing) use of metaphor, simile or personification to create vivid images in the reader’s mind, and so on. If time allows, ask students to identify three or more of their language choices and write a sentence or two about each one, explaining their choice and its intended effect.
  • Ask students to swap their writing so far with a partner.
  • Invite volunteers to share the first sentence of their partner’s description with the class.
  • Can you identify the writer’s intention? What mood is the writer trying to create? Ensure that suggestions are supported with reference to the text: the words and phrases that contribute to the mood created.
  • Ask students to complete the descriptive writing that they began in Student work 2.

Additional activities

  • If splitting the content of this lesson into two shorter sessions, these additional activities can be used to begin and end the sessions.

Setting and mood

  • If splitting the lesson to focus on figurative language in prose in the first session, and a sense of place in the second, this activity can be used to begin the second session.
  • Ask students to think of all the films or television programmes they have seen recently, and all the novels or short stories they have read.
  • Ask students to identify one or more settings from one or more of those films, stories or television programmes, and the mood created in that setting: how did it make them feel as they read or watched?
  • Note responses on the board for reference in the second session.
  • If splitting the lesson to focus on figurative language in prose in the first session, and a sense of place in the second, this activity can be used to conclude the first session.
  • Remind students: Effective description uses the five senses – sight, sound, smell, taste and touch – to convey vivid impressions of people and places to the reader.
  • Ask students to identify which of the five senses are used in the extract from Lord of the Flies. (Answer: Sight is used predominantly; the ‘heat’ and the ‘coarse’ grass convey texture/touch.)
  • Ask students to suggest features of the island that the writer of Lord of the Flies could have described using all five senses. (Possible answers: the sound of the ocean; the feel of the breeze; the smell of decaying coconuts; the taste of salty air)
