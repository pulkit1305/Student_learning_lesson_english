Lesson 69 | Teacher guidance Creating Character

  • While all care is taken to ensure resource content is in line with cultural sensitivities, please be aware that it should be reviewed before sharing with students.

Character and inference

  • Display the slideshow.
  • Explain to students: One method of creating a character in a story is to imagine what they might be carrying about their person – in a pocket or handbag, for example. The slide shows two characters’ belongings, carried on their person. What can you infer about each one?
  • Give students a minute to discuss with a partner and note their ideas.
  • Take feedback, ensuring students justify their ideas. 
  • Possible answers:
    • Character A: young (sweets, little money); intellectual (chess pieces); untidy (crumpled paper, broken pencil)
    • Character B: wealthy (expensive phone); vain (mirror, comb, sunglasses); business person (USB stick).

Characters in fiction

  • Read aloud the extract on pp. 214–15 of the Student Book, from Let’s Give It Up for Gimme Lao! by Sebastian Sim.
  • Ask students to note three words to describe the character of Grandma Toh. 
  • Take feedback to share ideas. Emphasise to students the importance of gathering as many descriptive words as possible, including synonyms, to create a precise response to character. Note that students need not justify their responses – they will do this in Student work 1. (Possible answers: unkind, brutal, ruthless, firm, heartless, selfish)
  • Explain to students: The questions in Activity 2 on p. 215 of the Student Book will help you to develop your response to the character of Grandma Toh.
  • Ask students to review the extract again and then answer the questions.
  • Take feedback to share ideas and develop understanding. 
  • Possible answers:
    • Q2. The writer gives little description of her appearance (‘her ample bosom’) but focuses mainly on her actions and on Gimme Lao’s reactions to her. The writer does not give Grandma Toh dialogue and, therefore, how she says it is not described.
    • Q3. Grandma Toh’s silence arguably makes her all the more unsympathetic. Gimme Lao’s dislike of Grandma Toh suggests she is an unsympathetic character.
    • Q4. Grandma Toh’s unkindness is shown when she forces Gimme to do things he does not want or need to do, such as eat, bathe and sleep when not hungry, dirty or tired; (1) when she feeds him roughly ‘with an aluminium spoon so large it stretched his lips’; (2) when she washes him with a rough towel making his skin ‘raw and red’; (3) when she tears him ‘wailing and clawing’ away from his parents each morning; (4) when she pinches him ‘with the sharp ends of her nails’; (5) Grandma Toh’s selfishness is suggested when she makes Gimme lie down so she can listen to her favourite radio show and would ‘shush him fiercely’ if he ‘fussed’.

Support

For students who struggle with characters in fiction:

  • Suggest students start their work on Activity 2 with question 3: noting five things that the writer shows or describes about Grandma Toh and considering the impression they create.
  • Students can then consider which of the writer’s language choices helped to create each impression.
  • Students can then consider which of the five given techniques the writer has or has not used and respond to questions 1 and 2.

Characters in poetry

  • Explain to students: Characters are created in fiction – but also in poetry. We are going to explore how one poet creates character in their poem ‘In the Middle of Dinner.’
  • Read aloud the poem by Chris Abani on p. 217 of the Student Book.
  • Invite students’ responses to the poem and to the character of the mother created in the poem. You may want to invite volunteers to clarify the setting and the events of the poem to support the class’s understanding: the speaker describes his mother removing her wedding ring from her wedding ring finger, where it has been for so long it has left a ‘groove’, and putting it on a different finger. She explains her actions: she wrote to her husband frequently but has never received a reply.
  • Ask students to note three words to describe the character of the mother.
  • Take feedback to share ideas. Note that students need not justify their responses – they will do this in Student work 2. (Possible answers: quiet, calm, relieved, unconcerned)
  • Ask students: Which of the five techniques for creating character (shown on p. 216) has the poet used to create the character of the mother? (Answer: what she does, what she says)
  • Highlight the lack of description of appearance in either the poem or the prose fiction extract from Let’s Give It Up for Gimme Lao! Point out that, while description of physical appearance may help the writer to picture a character, it is not necessary for creating a vivid impression of character.
  • Ask students to complete Activity 4 on p. 216 of the Student Book to develop their understanding of the character of the mother in the poem ‘In the Middle of Dinner’.
  • Take feedback to share ideas and develop understanding. Possible answers: 
    • Q1. The speaker and his mother are eating dinner together.
    • Q2. The mother is presented as calm and without anger (‘contemplatively… natural… tender’). There is a sense of anger with her husband (‘five years… five years… time was like ash on my tongue… not a single note’), but a sense of relief that she has accepted the end of her marriage (‘sighed, smiling, the weight gone’).
    • Q3. The speaker’s father is presented as uncaring (‘five years… not a single note.’)
    • Q4. The final line, in particular, suggests strongly that the speaker’s mother is determined to forget the past and enjoy the present.
    • Q5. The poet may have written the poem as a record of a significant moment in his mother’s life or, perhaps, as an imagined scene showing the importance of moving on from damaging relationships.

Support

For students who struggle with characters in poetry:

  • Work with students as a whole class, or in a group, focusing on each line of the poem in turn and charting the impressions the speaker creates of his mother’s mood: from calm contemplation, to anger, to relief, to peace.
  • Having clarified these impressions, ask students to complete Activity 4.
  • Ask students to write a brief comparison of the two characters they have explored in the lesson – Grandma Toh and the mother in ‘In the Middle of Dinner’ – and the writer’s methods in creating them.
  • Students may note the brevity of the sympathetic characterisation of the mother – which nonetheless conveys a wealth of information about her and her relationship with her husband – and contrast it with the more detailed antithetic characterisation of Grandma Toh. Both, however, create a vivid impression of character.

Techniques of characterisation

  • Display the slideshow.
  • Ask students to look at the five techniques of characterisation shown on the slideshow.
  • Based on the techniques that the two writers used to create the two characters students have explored in this lesson, and the vividness of their impressions of those characters, ask students to rank the five techniques in order of effectiveness.
  • Take feedback. Accept any valid responses. Aim to highlight the value of all five techniques – and the importance of looking for a range of techniques the writer has used when analysing a text.
  • Distribute the homework sheet.
  • Ask students to complete the homework sheet, which supports students to write their own vivid character description, using some or all of the techniques of characterisation that they have explored in the lesson. Students can plan and write their response on the homework sheet.

Additional activities

  • If splitting the content of this lesson into two shorter sessions, these additional activities can be used to begin and end the sessions.

Techniques of characterisation

  • If splitting the lesson to focus on characters in fiction in the first session, and characters in poetry in the second, this activity can be used to begin the second session.
  • To check and refresh their memories, ask students to note down the five techniques of characterisation they explored in the first session – without referring to their notes from the first session or the Student Book!
  • Ask students to swap their responses with a partner.
  • Display the slideshow from the End-of-lesson activity again.
  • Ask students to check how many of the techniques their partner remembered.
  • Take feedback by asking students to hold up the same number of fingers as the number of techniques their partner noted.
  • If splitting the lesson to focus on characters in fiction in the first session, and characters in poetry in the second, this activity can be used to conclude the first session.
  • Invite students to consider and share their impressions of the character of Gimme Lao in the extract from Let’s Give it Up for Gimme Lao. (Possible answers: a helpless victim or, perhaps, a difficult and uncooperative child)
  • Ask students: Which of the five techniques of characterisation, (listed in Activity 2 on p. 215 of the Student Book), has the writer used to create these impressions? (Answers: his actions; other characters’ reactions)
